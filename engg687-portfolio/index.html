Engineering Ethics & Responsibility

Summarizing my biggest takeaway about ethics and professional responsibility from ENGG 687.

3

My biggest takeaway from ENGG 687 is that ethics in engineering mainly comes down to designing for safety right from the start. It should not be added later or treated like an extra step. I learned that being a responsible engineer means thinking ahead, looking for possible risks, and not being afraid to question assumptions. We always have to keep people’s safety and well-being above cost or any pressure we might face. This course showed me that safety is not only a technical thing, but also an ethical duty that needs clear communication and proper documentation.

Going into my career, I want to use these learnings by checking for hazards early, talking openly with my team, and making sure that every design choice considers how it affects people in the long run. ENGG 687 really taught me that safety must always come first.

One Lecture That Shifted My Perspective

Reflecting on a lecture topic Challenger Space Shuttle Disaster

4

One thing that really surprised me in the lecture was how much leadership, communication, and emotions can affect engineering ethics. I always thought failures were mostly technical, but the Challenger case showed that poor communication between different management levels and pressure to “just launch” were bigger issues than the O-ring itself. Seeing how Level 3 warnings never reached Level 1 and 2 managers really changed how I look at responsibility in organizations.

To go deeper, I read more about how engineers tried to speak up but felt ignored, which made me think about how hard it can be to challenge decisions. It also made me question why safety concerns were treated like obstacles instead of priorities. This challenged my thinking, and now I see how important open discussion and honest reporting are in engineering teams.

Course Reading 1 · Reflection

Reflection on the video "What is Doughnut Economics?" - with Kate Raworth

5

Learning about Doughnut Economics from "Design to cost" lecture made me think differently about engineering and ethics because it shows that good engineering is not just about growth, but about staying within environmental limits while still meeting people’s basic needs. It helped me realize that ethics is not only about rules, but also about designing systems that don’t harm communities or the planet in the long run.

Comparing Canada and India, I noticed that Canada is generally closer to the “safe and just space” because it has stronger regulations, stable infrastructure, and more awareness of sustainability. India, on the other hand, is still working on balancing development with environmental protection, especially in fast-growing cities. I have personally seen projects in India where cost and speed were prioritized over long-term impacts. This always made me question if we could have designed things in a safer and more sustainable way.

Course Reading 2 · Reflection

Reflection on the video "The Iron Ring - What it Means to be a Canadian Engineer"

6

Learning about the Iron Ring Ceremony from "Iron Ring Ceremony" Lecture in Canada made me think about engineering ethics in a much deeper way. The ceremony shows that engineering is not just a technical job, but a responsibility to society. What stood out to me is that the ring acts as a constant reminder to make safe, honest, and thoughtful decisions, even when there is pressure or deadlines. The idea that engineers publicly commit to ethical practice made me reflect on how every design choice, even small ones, can impact people’s lives.

I also found it interesting that the ceremony focuses on humility and duty rather than celebrating achievement. It made me realize that being a good engineer means accepting accountability and always putting safety first. This changed the way I think about my future career, reminding me to stay responsible and grounded in my decisions.

Additional Suggested Material 1

An external resource I found and how it connects to an ENGG 687 topic.

7

Course topic: Lecture Design for Cost
Suggested material: YouTube video: “How Amazon Tricks You With Their Own Products”.

Brief summary: The video explains how Amazon uses its own platform to push Amazon Basics products by ranking them higher, even when they are more expensive and lower in quality than other brands. It shows how the company prioritizes its own profit instead of fairness or transparency.

Reflection:This resource changed my thinking because it showed how big companies can ignore their own core values like “earn trust” and “insist on highest standards.” Watching how Amazon promotes its own products over better options made me rethink what ethical responsibility looks like in large tech systems. It also made me reflect on engineering decisions, how bias in algorithms, ranking systems, or product placement can affect millions of customers. I disagree strongly with Amazon’s strategy because a platform should highlight good products, not just its own.

Additional Suggested Material 2

An external resource I found and how it connects to an ENGG 687 topic.

8

Course topic: DEsign for Equity
Suggested material: American Factory by Netflix

Brief summary: American Factory shows what happens when a Chinese company reopens a closed American factory and brings in its own management style. The film highlights the tension between two completely different work cultures—one focused on discipline and long hours, and the other focused on worker rights, safety, and fair treatment.

Reflection: What stood out to me was that the biggest conflict wasn’t the factory technology but the values behind the work. The Chinese leadership emphasized loyalty, speed, and efficiency, while the American workers cared more about dignity, safety, and having a voice through unions. This made me think a lot about ethics because it shows how engineering decisions don’t exist in a vacuum—they are shaped by culture, expectations, and human needs. For me, the main lesson is that technical success means nothing if people feel unsafe or unheard. As a future engineer, I need to respect different perspectives and always put human values before efficiency.

Looking Back 1 · Early Reflection

Re-visiting one of my early reflection quiz entries on ethical use of Gen AI.

10

When I look back at my reflections, my relationship with Gen AI has been a mix of trial, frustration, and learning. I still remember writing, “Well from that day I decided not using any Gen AI again in solving any mathematical problems.” That came from a real experience where AI basically failed on complex electrical problems. It showed me that blind trust in AI is dangerous, especially in a maths-intensive field like electrical engineering.

At the same time, I also wrote, “I have used AI for Job search, Writing resumes for me, Writing coffee chat messges for me, and much more.” So for me, ethical use of Gen AI is about where and how I use it. I avoid it for core learning and problem-solving, but I do use it for support tasks like formatting, emails, and understanding code, always trying to stay honest and transparent.
